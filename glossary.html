Campus DRI Glossary

Alpha · Academic DRIs · 2026–27
Reference guide for newer team members. Terms are defined as Alpha uses them — not general education definitions.
This glossary defines the key terms used across the Campus DRI role and the TimeBack platform. If you hear a term in a meeting or see it in the dashboard and aren't sure what it means in our context, start here.
People & Roles

DRI — Directly Responsible Individual

The single person who owns the outcome for a specific area. In our context, the Campus DRI owns academic outcomes for every student at their assigned campus. Not a monitor — an owner. If something is broken, the DRI finds it and fixes it.

Example: If a student has had no content for three days, the Campus DRI is responsible for identifying and resolving it — not waiting to be told.

Guide

An Alpha staff member who supports students during their 2-hour academic learning sessions. Guides are not teachers — they do not instruct. Their role is to facilitate, encourage focus, and flag concerns. The Campus DRI's primary partner for student interventions.

Example: A guide notices a student has been on the same lesson for 40 minutes. The Campus DRI flags this, reviews the recording, and tells the guide how to support the student.

Lead Guide

The senior guide at a campus — the Campus DRI's main point of contact for day-to-day coordination. The Lead Guide communicates with the broader guide team and is the first person the DRI reaches out to when flagging patterns or requesting action on a group of students.

Campus

An individual Alpha school location. Each Campus DRI is assigned one or more campuses and is solely accountable for all academic outcomes at those sites. When you hear "your campus," it means the specific school(s) you have been assigned — not Alpha as a whole.

Example: A Campus DRI might be assigned to Miami and Austin. They own academic monitoring for every student across both sites.

Curriculum DRI

The person who owns the content and progression logic for a specific subject (e.g., Math K–8, Language, Reading). When the Campus DRI has a subject-level question — about app assignments, XP rules, or content gaps — they escalate to the Curriculum DRI for that subject.

Student Status & Flags

Red / Red Flag

A student metric that signals a significant problem requiring a documented action within 24 hours. A red is not a concern to monitor — it is a trigger to act.

Common reds:

  • No activity for 48+ hours on a school day
  • Zero XP for multiple consecutive days
  • Accuracy below 60% across 3+ days
  • Doom loop — same test failed 3+ times without resolution
  • Student has no content assigned (empty plan)

Example: A student logs in but earns 0 XP for three straight days. That is a red. You document it, find the root cause, and notify the guide with a plan — all within 24 hours.

False Green

A student who looks on track based on surface metrics — they are logging time, earning XP, and showing no obvious alerts — but whose actual learning is ineffective. The numbers are green, but the learning is not happening.

False greens are caused by anti-patterns: rushing through lessons, guessing without reading, watching videos on fast-forward, completing content without understanding it, or cycling through easy material for quick XP.

Example: A student earns 30 XP in 10 minutes. That looks great — until you watch the recording and see they clicked through every question in under 3 seconds. The XP is real; the learning is not.

How to spot it: Check XP/minute efficiency, look at accuracy trends, and always verify with StudyReel recordings before concluding a student is genuinely progressing.

Doom Loop

When a student repeatedly attempts the same test or lesson without passing, cycling in place without making progress. A doom loop is always a red flag. It means the student is missing a prerequisite skill, is not learning from their mistakes, or has an anti-pattern preventing genuine engagement.

Example: A student takes the Grade 4 Math test three times in a week, scores 45–55% each time, and keeps getting reassigned to the same test. That is a doom loop. Action required: pre-test coaching call with the guide + custom plan from the Subject DRI.

Anti-Pattern

A recurring student behavior that signals learning is not actually happening, even when the platform records activity. Anti-patterns waste time, inflate XP, and mask real knowledge gaps.

The six main anti-patterns at Alpha:

  • Rushing — clicking through content without reading or thinking
  • Skipping explanations — moving on immediately after a wrong answer
  • Guessing — very short response times, low accuracy, high attempt count
  • Skipping instructional material — going straight to quizzes without watching the lesson
  • Idling — session time logged with long inactive gaps
  • Gaming the system — repeating easy content to earn XP without genuine challenge
Academic Structure & Progression

Test (Placement Test)

A subject-specific assessment that determines where a student belongs in the curriculum. Tests are not graded for transcripts — they are diagnostic tools that drive the student's next assignment. Every student starts with a placement test when they join and receives new tests each time they advance a grade level.

How test scores are used (Bracketing Logic):

  • Below 60% (below 80% for K–3 Math) → Full skill plan for that grade
  • 60–90% → Hole filling — only the missed skills
  • Over 90% → Advance to the next grade test

Example: A student takes the Grade 3 Math test and scores 72%. They don't repeat the whole grade — they receive hole filling that targets only the questions they got wrong.

Hole Filling

Targeted remediation assigned when a student scores between 60% and 90% on a placement test. Instead of repeating the entire grade's content, the student only works on the specific skills they missed. Hole filling is faster and more efficient than a full skill plan.

Example: A student scores 78% on Grade 5 Language. They didn't miss everything — hole filling assigns lessons only for the standards they got wrong. Once complete, they test out and move to Grade 6.

Full Skill Plan

A comprehensive remediation plan assigned when a student scores below 60% on a test (below 80% for K–3 Math). The student works through all the skills for that grade level in sequence before retesting. More thorough than hole filling, and takes longer to complete.

Bracketing

The process of placing a student at the correct working level based on their test scores. Bracketing uses the scoring thresholds (below 60% / 60–90% / over 90%) to determine what comes next. The goal is to always have students working at their actual level — not too easy, not out of reach.

Example: A new student joins at age-grade 6 and takes the Grade 3 test (the starting point for students 6th grade and above). They score 92% → they advance directly to the Grade 4 test. That is bracketing moving them up efficiently.

Age Grade

The grade level that corresponds to a student's age — what grade they would be in at a traditional school. Used to determine starting points (students below 6th age-grade start at Kinder; 6th and above start at 3rd grade). Age grade is not the same as the student's working level — many Alpha students work well above or below their age grade.

Mastered Grade

The highest grade level a student has successfully demonstrated mastery of through a subject test (scoring above 90%). This is the key measure of academic progress — not time spent, not XP, but grade levels actually mastered.

Example: A 4th-grade-age student who has mastered up to Grade 6 Math has a mastered grade of 6 in Math — two years ahead. That is 2× growth.

2× Growth

Alpha's core academic commitment: every student advances at least two grade levels per academic year, as measured by MAP assessments and subject mastery. This is not a stretch goal — it is the baseline expectation. A student who grows only one grade level in a year has not met the standard.

MAP (Measures of Academic Progress)

Standardized assessments administered at the start of the year, mid-year (Winter MAP), and end of year (Spring MAP). MAP scores measure actual grade-level performance and are the primary external benchmark for academic growth. MAP results drive end-of-year evaluations and help identify which students need intensified support before the next test window.

Literably / ORF Test

An Oral Reading Fluency test required for K–2 students between each mastered reading grade level. A student must pass their Literably test before advancing to the next reading grade — even if they have already scored above 90% on the grade-level reading test. These are assigned automatically; if one does not appear, contact the early literacy team.

Platform & Metrics

XP (Experience Points)

The metric TimeBack uses to measure academic output. Every completed lesson, quiz, or activity earns a set number of XP based on the platform and accuracy threshold. XP is the primary daily progress metric — but high XP alone does not mean genuine learning (see: False Green).

Daily target: 125 XP total across all subjects. Per subject: 25 XP for Math, Language, Reading, Writing/Science; 10 XP for Fast Math.

Important: XP is not awarded for activity completed below the accuracy threshold. A student who finishes a lesson at 60% accuracy may earn reduced or zero XP. Use the ⓘ icon in TimeBack's learning metrics to understand why XP was or wasn't awarded.

Productive Time vs. Unproductive Time

Productive time is time spent actively engaged with content at the expected pace and accuracy — lessons being watched, questions being read carefully, answers being considered. Unproductive time is session time that is logged but does not result in learning: idling, rushing, app switching, or sitting on a screen without engaging. The goal is not just high total minutes — it is high productive minutes.

Rule of thumb: If a student spends 25 minutes in Reading but earns 2 XP, the vast majority of that time is unproductive. Investigate why.

Accuracy

The percentage of questions a student answers correctly within a session or subject. Accuracy is a two-sided signal: too low means the student is struggling or guessing; too high may mean the content is too easy or the student is gaming easy material.

Ideal range: 80–90%. Below 80% for 3+ consecutive days → flag and investigate. Above 90% consistently → consider whether the student is ready to test out.

Deep Dive

A structured, in-depth analysis of a single student's data, done when a significant recurring pattern is preventing learning. A deep dive goes beyond daily monitoring — it identifies the root cause, documents findings, and ends with a specific action plan assigned to the student, the guide, and the Campus DRI. Not every student needs a deep dive — reserve it for cases where daily flags have not resolved the issue.

See examples: Full academic deep dive · Single-subject deep dive (links in playbook)

StudyReel

The recording platform that captures students' on-screen sessions during learning time. StudyReel is the Campus DRI's most important verification tool — when data shows a concern, always check the recording before drawing conclusions or escalating. It is the difference between flagging a real problem and flagging a platform glitch.

Example: A student shows 3 minutes of XP in 45 minutes of logged time. Before escalating, check StudyReel. It may show the student was on a loading screen the whole time (platform issue) — or it may show them scrolling social media (guide intervention needed).

TimeBack Dash

The primary monitoring platform used by the Campus DRI. Shows XP, time on task, accuracy, grade-level progress, test history, doom loops, notes, and messaging for every student. Your main tool — open it every morning and use it to prioritise your day.

Test Out Sheet

A shared Google Sheet that tracks all placement test completions and their follow-up assignments. Check it daily. If a student has completed a test and no next step has been assigned (hole filling, full plan, or next test), the Test Out Sheet is where you flag it or request action.

Process Terms

Escalation

Formally raising an issue to the appropriate contact when it cannot be resolved at the Campus DRI level. Always escalate with: student name, campus, clear summary of the issue, supporting evidence, and the specific action you are requesting. Escalation is not a failure — it is how the system works. The failure is not escalating when you should.

Support Ticket

A formal request submitted through Slack (TB Support — Academics chat) to resolve a platform or automation issue. Required any time a student-facing automation has failed — missing plans, wrong enrolments, tests not assigned. Contacting a DRI directly may get a faster fix, but a support ticket is still required to track the issue.

Weekly Analysis

A structured review done at least once per week where the Campus DRI evaluates each student's trajectory — not just what happened that day, but whether they are on track for their next milestone (MAP test, grade-level mastery, placement test). If goals were met, look for acceleration opportunities. If not met, identify root cause and adjust the plan.

Intervention

Any action taken in response to a student concern — from a quick guide flag in Slack to a full deep dive and custom plan. Every intervention must be logged. Undocumented interventions cannot be tracked, escalated, or reviewed. If it happened but wasn't logged, it didn't happen.
