Purpose

The Campus DRI is responsible for ensuring every student makes measurable academic progress, supporting guides through data interpretation, placement management, testing coordination, and targeted interventions. The goal is simple:

  • Students are always working at the appropriate level.
  • Academic issues are identified early.
  • Guides know exactly how to support their students.
  • Families receive accurate, timely information.
  • Escalations happen before they become problems.
Role scope: You own academic outcomes across all subjects and students at your assigned campuses. As the single point of academic accountability, you are responsible for identifying issues, removing blockers, coordinating with the appropriate teams, and ensuring guides and students have everything they need to make academic progress.

Success Criteria

You are doing your job when your campus achieves all three of the following:

# Criterion What it means
1 2× Academic Growth Students grow at least two grade levels per academic year, as measured by MAP and subject assessments.
2 Targeted Remediation Identified skill gaps are addressed through the correct plan — hole filling, full skill plan, or custom placement — not left open.
3 Grade-Level Proficiency Students reach grade-level benchmarks on subject assessments within their enrolled year.

Rules to Stand By

These are non-negotiable operating principles for the Campus DRI role.

# Rule What it means in practice
1 Use apps first-hand Log in as a student. Experience the platform before advising on it. Never diagnose what you haven't seen yourself.
2 Own the outcome You are accountable for student progress — not just for monitoring it. If a student is stuck, it is your responsibility to move them forward.
3 Run a single decluttered tracker One source of truth. Scattered notes and informal tracking create gaps. Everything goes in the same place.
4 Spot false greens Green metrics can mask real problems — rushing, guessing, gaming XP. Always verify the quality of activity, not just the quantity.
5 Act within 24 hours on reds Any red flag — no activity, repeated test failures, zero XP — gets a documented action within one business day.
6 Guide communication is collaborative Bring guides into your analysis. They are your partners, not just the recipients of your flags. You are a team.
7 Log every issue If it's not documented, it didn't happen. Undocumented interventions cannot be escalated, tracked, or reviewed.

03 · The Student's Test Journey (TimeBack)

Understanding where a student is in the system is your first step before any intervention.

Onboarding → First Test

Students begin by completing MAP tests (handled by Thiago De Sa and Guilherme Colombo). Once MAPs are complete, students receive their first subject test:

  • Below 6th grade age-grade → starts with the Kinder (K) test
  • 6th grade or above → starts with the 3rd grade test

These tests can be found on the Placement page in the student's individual TimeBack profile.

Bracketing Logic

Score Next Step
Below 60% (below 80% for K–3 Math) Full skill plan
60–90% Hole filling
Over 90% Next grade test
First Test Exception: If the student's first test is not a Kinder test and they fail it, they receive a Kinder test next before entering the normal path. This applies to the first test only.
Example: First test is 3rd grade, student scores 80% → they receive a Kinder test next, then proceed upward.

Placement Process

Confirm students are working at the correct grade level, receiving appropriate placement tests, and progressing normally through placement pathways. If placement seems incorrect:

  1. Verify completed coursework.
  2. Review previous assessment data.
  3. Confirm the testing sequence.
  4. Escalate when necessary.
Never request unnecessary placement changes without supporting evidence.

What to Check Daily

Tests Completed

  • Below 60% (below 80% for K–3 Math): Confirm a full plan was assigned. If not, open a support ticket and flag in the TB Support — Academics chat. If the student has already completed a full plan previously, confirm hole filling is assigned instead.
  • 60–90%: Confirm hole filling was assigned. If not, open a support ticket and flag in the TB Support — Academics chat.
  • Over 90%: Confirm the next grade test was assigned. If not, request it via the Test Out Sheet.

Note: Reaching out directly to the Curriculum DRI may result in a faster resolution, but a support ticket is still required to track the issue.

Literably Tests

  • K–2 students in Reading require a Literably ORF (Oral Reading Fluency) test before advancing to the next grade, even after mastering the grade-level test. These are assigned automatically. If one does not appear, contact the early literacy team to assign it.

Plan / Hole Filling Completion

  • Most assignments are automated. If a student runs out of content, check their TimeBack and app, then open a support ticket requesting the content to be added, and flag in the TB Support — Academics chat.

Assessment Support

Before requesting or assigning tests, verify:

  • The student has completed prerequisite work (hole filling or full skill plan).
  • Relevant benchmarks have been met (applicable to Writing, Vocabulary, and Science).
  • The student is academically ready.
  • The test is appropriate for transcript needs (e.g., student is leaving Alpha and needs grade-level results).

Examples: Alpha placements · Science benchmarks · Reading placements · Math placements · Grade-level assessments.

04 · Daily Monitoring Routine

Morning Check

Open TimeBack Dash (or your preferred platform) and review your campus student list. Prioritise by urgency, not alphabetical order. Flag any student where:

  • XP or time on task is below 80% of the expected daily target
  • A student is cycling the same test without passing (doom loop) — reach out to the guide for a pre/post-test coaching call to identify gaps, then contact the Subject DRI to request a custom plan
  • No activity in 48+ hours on a school day
  • Accuracy is below 60% across recent sessions (3 consecutive days is a reliable indicator)
  • A previous intervention is due for follow-up — confirm whether the issue has been resolved

What to Evaluate Per Student

Time

  • Daily time on task — flag to the guide if below 50% of expected
  • Weekly consistency — flag if a student is consistently below 80% of required weekly time
  • Trends over time

Productivity

  • Productive vs. unproductive time
  • Time spent idle
  • Excessive app switching
  • Long inactive periods

Academic Progress

  • Lessons completed
  • XP earned — below 80% for 3 consecutive days warrants investigation
  • Accuracy — the ideal range is 80–90%. Below 80% indicates struggle; above 90% may indicate the content is too easy
  • Pace of progression
  • Placement movement

Anti-Patterns to Watch

Anti-Pattern What It Looks Like in TimeBack
Rushing through content Short session times relative to XP earned; high attempt rate with low dwell time
Skipping explanations after errors Wrong answer followed immediately by the next attempt, with no time spent on the correction
Guessing / clicking through Very short response times; high attempt count, low accuracy
Skipping instructional material Quiz attempts with no lesson view beforehand
Idling / off-task Long gaps of inactivity mid-session; session time logged without corresponding activity
Gaming the system Retaking easy or already-mastered content for XP; cycling low-level content repeatedly
When you spot an anti-pattern: Note it in the student's Notes → Flag the guide in Slack with the specific data point → If the pattern repeats, escalate to the Subject DRI.

Intervention Decision Tree

Student is behind on time

Diagnose: Attendance issue? Low motivation? Technical problem? Does the student understand the expectations?

Action: Contact the guide · Build a recovery plan · Monitor to confirm time increases.

Student has low productivity (low XP, high time)

Diagnose: Distracted? Constant app or tab switching? Off-task?

Action: Review StudyReel footage · Flag the guide · Request a coaching call to help the student build focus habits.

Student progressing slowly

Diagnose: Knowledge gap? Reading comprehension issue? Low confidence? Avoiding difficult content?

Action: Flag for a guide coaching session · Scaffold lessons if needed · Set small, achievable goals.

Student progressing too quickly with low accuracy

Diagnose: Guessing? Skipping explanations? Rushing assessments?

Action: Slow the student down before allowing further acceleration. Flag the guide immediately.

Weekly Analysis

At least once per week, step back from daily monitoring and evaluate each student's trajectory. The question is not just "what happened today?" but "are they on track for their next milestone?"

If weekly goals were met

  • Confirm the student is on track for the next MAP or assessment milestone
  • Look for opportunities to accelerate — are they ready for the next test?

If weekly goals were not met

  • Identify the root cause before acting: placement issue? attendance? engagement? technical problem? knowledge gap?
  • Adjust the plan: add content, reassign, contact the DRI, or escalate
  • Set a revised target for the following week — don't leave it open-ended
  • Document the change and notify the guide of unmet expectations and your plan going forward
A student who misses their weekly goal two weeks in a row requires an escalation note, not just a flag. Patterns need action.

Deep Dive Process

Deep Dives should be reserved for cases where a significant, recurring pattern is preventing a student from learning effectively. Most daily and weekly checks do not require this level of depth.

When conducting a Deep Dive, structure your analysis around three areas:

Issues

Time · Productivity · Accuracy · Behavioural patterns

Root Cause

Avoid treating symptoms. Ask: Why is this happening? Common root causes: reading comprehension · motivation · missing prerequisite knowledge · attendance · technical issues.

Action Plan

Every Deep Dive ends with clear, assigned next steps:

  • Student action
  • Guide action
  • Campus DRI follow-up
  • Timeline for review
See examples: Full academic deep dive · Single-subject deep dive

05 · Subject-Specific Support

The most common issues to watch for per subject when reviewing student data or flagging for intervention.

Reading

Watch for: skipping instructions · guessing · reading only part of the question or passage · vocabulary gaps.

When flagging to a guide: direct students to read the passage twice, highlight key information, and explain what the question is asking before selecting an answer.

Math

Common signals in the data: misreading questions · ignoring units · missing keywords · place value confusion · difficulty with multi-step problems · no written working.

Interventions should focus on reasoning, not just computation — breaking problems apart, identifying what is known and what needs to be found.

Science

Watch for: vocabulary misunderstandings · difficulty interpreting diagrams · limited background knowledge.

Interventions: direct students to read captions first, examine visuals before attempting questions, and apply process of elimination.

06 · Guide Support

Flagging Issues

When raising concerns with a guide, lead with specific observations rather than conclusions. Be collaborative — you are working toward the same goal.

Instead of: "Your student isn't learning."

Try: "I noticed they're submitting answers very quickly without reading the explanation after errors. It would be worth slowing them down and asking them to explain their thinking before submitting."

Or: "Their accuracy has been below 70% for three days in a row. It looks like they're struggling with this lesson — would they be open to a coaching session on it?"

07 · Escalation Guidelines

When to Escalate

  • A student cannot progress due to a platform issue
  • Placement appears incorrect
  • Testing requests fall outside normal processes
  • Technical problems are preventing learning
  • A student requires curriculum review
  • A parent concern requires additional support

How to Escalate

Always escalate with: student name · campus · clear summary · supporting evidence · requested action.

Best practices: Look for trends, not isolated events. Verify the issue before escalating. Document every intervention. Always follow up to confirm the action was taken.

08 · TimeBack FAQ

No recommendations available for a student

  • Just completed a test: Recommendations run once per day. Knowledge gaps appear the next day.
  • Just completed knowledge gaps: Check the DeepDive Dashboard for the last lesson completed. New content will appear in the next cycle.
  • No reading content, rostered in K–2: Request a Lalilo account from Nick Alsford.
  • None of the above: Open an immediate support ticket.

MobyMax — no recommendations available

Check the student's MobyMax activity, sorted by most recently completed. If content is not automatically reassigned within 15 minutes, reassign manually:

  1. Open the student's MobyMax link → locate the course to reassign
  2. Sort lessons by accuracy → select all below 80%
  3. Click "Assign Remediation Skills" → name it "Reassign Hole-Filling. [Subject] [Student Name]"
  4. Always prefer automation — only reassign manually if 15 minutes have passed with no update

Freckle (Language): If empty, contact Barbara Franks.

Student wants to test out of their grade

  • Currently doing knowledge gaps: The student must complete all knowledge gaps before a new test can be assigned.
  • Rostered, scored below 60%: Confirm with the Curriculum DRI before making any change. Each student is allowed one test-out attempt.
  • Rostered in K–3 Math, scored 60–80%: Discuss with the DRI — options include Zearn placements from G3 or Zearn Missions (K–2). One test-out attempt allowed.

Student getting repeated lessons

  • Full plan: Check whether all lessons for that grade have been completed — this may be a bug that restarted the plan from the beginning.
  • Knowledge gaps: Usually means the student failed the same type of question again on their most recent test.

Test appears in TimeBack but not in Alphatest

This usually means the test was marked as absent. Verify in Alphatest and open a support ticket to reassign. Ping Davi Deconti for a quick fix.

Student is rostered but failed the test intentionally

Contact Davi Deconti. A new test will be reassigned and progression should normalise afterwards.

High school students still working through K–8 content

High school students must still complete K–8 content before accessing HS-level material. The goal is to accelerate their progression as efficiently as possible while providing strong support to their guides throughout.

Guide doesn't understand why a student didn't earn XP

This is a common question. The first step is to review the student's learning metrics in TimeBack — use the info icon (ⓘ) next to each activity to understand why XP was or wasn't awarded. If the recording confirms the student should have received XP and the issue is unclear, open a support ticket and flag it in the TB Support — Academics chat.

Success Metrics

A successful Campus DRI looks like this:

  • Students remain appropriately placed and are progressing at the expected pace.
  • Guides feel informed, supported, and confident in their decisions.
  • Academic issues are identified and addressed before they escalate.
  • Families trust the process and receive clear, accurate communication.
  • Students become increasingly independent, self-directed learners.
  • Interventions lead to measurable improvement — not short-term fixes.

Remember: your guides are your team. Work together to make sure every student learns and every student succeeds.
